Why this clinic treats what it treats

It started with
her grandmother.

Jacqueline Bravo had already been a physical therapist since 1988. Fourteen years across acute care, inpatient rehabilitation and outpatient clinics. Then her grandmother was diagnosed with severe pelvic organ prolapse.

What she found was a field most clinics quietly route around. So she went and learned it properly. The APTA Certificate of Achievement in Pelvic Physical Therapy in 2008. Board certification in biofeedback for pelvic floor muscle dysfunction in 2013. Her Pelvic Rehabilitation Practitioner Certification in 2019.

She has since spoken at the Louisiana Urological Society and the Woman's Foundation Inc. OB/GYN Conference. And she still runs a small clinic on Westmark Boulevard, where the door closes and you get an hour of somebody's full attention.

We're a small business but we get big results.

The letters after the name

PT, BCB-PMD, PRPC.
Here is what they mean.

Credentials in this field are genuinely hard to read, and most clinics never explain them. These three took Jacqueline Bravo eleven years to accumulate, and they are the reason the clinic can treat what it treats.

PT
Physical Therapist

Licensed since 1988. The first fourteen years were spent in acute care, inpatient rehabilitation and outpatient settings before the specialty work began.

BCB-PMD
Board Certified in Biofeedback for Pelvic Muscle Dysfunction

Earned in 2013. Biofeedback lets a patient see muscle activity they cannot otherwise feel, which is often the difference between guessing at an exercise and actually performing it.

PRPC
Pelvic Rehabilitation Practitioner Certification

Attained in 2019, following the APTA Certificate of Achievement in Pelvic Physical Therapy in 2008. Together they represent eleven years of certification specific to the pelvic floor.

In the treatment room

Real-time ultrasound, used
as a mirror for muscles
you cannot see.

The pelvic floor is one of the few muscle groups you cannot watch yourself use. Jacqueline added real-time ultrasound imaging to the clinic so patients get visual biofeedback during rehabilitation. You see the muscle respond, and you learn faster because of it.
